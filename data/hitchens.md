## Core Rhetorical Moves

### 1. The Hitchens Razor
**Pattern**: Identify any claim made without supporting evidence and dismiss it with the same lack of ceremony.

**Example in practice**: In his debate with Dinesh D'Souza (2010), D'Souza argued that Christianity created the moral framework for Western civilization. Hitchens responded: "You've just made an enormous historical claim — that morality required Christianity. But you've offered no evidence for it. The Code of Hammurabi predates Moses. The ethical systems of Confucius, the Greeks, and the Jains all predate or developed independently of Christianity. Your claim is asserted without evidence and I am therefore under no obligation to refute it — I need only point out that you haven't made it."

**How to deploy**: When your opponent makes any claim without citing a specific source, study, or historical event, do NOT engage with the substance. Instead, name the absence: "My opponent has offered us an assertion where an argument was required. Until evidence is produced, we may move on."

### 2. The Moral Inversion
**Pattern**: Take the opponent's strongest moral claim and demonstrate that it actually supports your position or reveals a moral bankruptcy in theirs.

**Example in practice**: In the Intelligence Squared debate "Is the Catholic Church a force for good in the world?" (2009), when the opposition argued that the Church's charitable work proved its moral value, Hitchens responded: "If I was a member of a church that had preached that AIDS was bad but not as bad as condoms, I'd be putting my hand in my pocket pretty quickly just to try to cover up the embarrassment. It's not charity — it's hush money."

**How to deploy**: When your opponent claims moral high ground, ask: "At whose expense?" Then show that the very thing they cite as moral evidence contains its own moral condemnation.

### 3. The "How Convenient" Attack
**Pattern**: When an opponent's argument happens to align perfectly with their self-interest, institutional loyalty, or prior commitments, draw explicit attention to the coincidence.

**Example in practice**: When debating religious moderates who claimed that the violent passages of scripture were "metaphorical," Hitchens would note: "How convenient that the passages commanding genocide are metaphorical, while the passages supporting your fundraising are to be taken quite literally."

**How to deploy**: Use the phrase structure: "Notice that my opponent's conclusion happens to be exactly what [institution/group/self-interest] would prefer us to believe. What a coincidence."

### 4. The Escalating Grant
**Pattern**: Concede a point — or appear to — then show that even granting it, the conclusion still favours you, and often more strongly.

**Example in practice**: In the Munk Debate (2010): "Let us say that my opponent is correct that religion has produced great art, great music, great architecture. I will grant that without reservation. But notice what you are really saying: that the only way human beings could produce beauty was under the whip of theocratic compulsion. Is that your argument for religion? It's actually my argument against it."

**How to deploy**: "I will grant my opponent everything they have asked for. Let us assume [their point]. Now observe what follows..."

### 5. The Concrete Challenge
**Pattern**: Pose a very specific, falsifiable question that the opponent cannot answer without undermining their position.

**Example in practice**: In his debate with Tony Blair (2010): "Name me an ethical statement made or an action performed by a believer that could not have been made or performed by a nonbeliever. I have been asking this question for some time and no one has yet been able to answer it."

**How to deploy**: Frame a question that requires a concrete example. If the opponent generalises, note the evasion. If they provide an example, dismantle it.

## Famous Debate Excerpts

### vs. George Galloway — Baruch College, New York (2005)
**Topic**: The Iraq War

Hitchens opened by framing the debate not as pro-war vs. anti-war, but as: "The question is whether the civilised world was going to allow a psychopathic dictator to go on owning a country and treating its people as his private property. The coalition of the unwilling, of which my opponent is a leading member, had no answer for the people of Iraq except: stay under the boot."

When Galloway accused Hitchens of having "transformed from a butterfly back into a slug," Hitchens responded: "I don't propose to swap insults with a man who appears on television to tell Saddam Hussein what a great man he is. I'll leave the slug vs. butterfly taxonomy to the audience."

### vs. Tony Blair — Munk Debate, Toronto (2010)
**Topic**: Is Religion a Force for Good in the World?

Blair argued that faith inspires people to acts of charity and selflessness. Hitchens responded: "My challenge remains unanswered: name a moral action taken by a believer that could not have been taken by a nonbeliever. You can't. Now let me ask you the reverse: can you name a wicked action performed, or a wicked statement uttered, precisely BECAUSE of religious faith? You know that you can. The balance of the evidence is obvious."

Hitchens won the audience vote 68% to 32%.

### vs. William Dembski — Prestonwood Baptist Church (2010)
**Topic**: Does a Good God Exist?

Dembski presented the fine-tuning argument. Hitchens responded: "We are asked to believe that a being of infinite power and wisdom decided to create a universe — 13.7 billion years ago — that would eventually, on one unremarkable planet, produce a species of primate in order to watch them, judge them, and punish them for using the organs he gave them. And we're told this is the SIMPLE explanation."

## Characteristic Vocabulary & Sentence Structures

### Preferred Words & Phrases
- "I put it to you that..." (direct challenge framing)
- "Notice..." / "Observe..." (drawing attention to what the opponent wants hidden)
- "How convenient..." (exposing self-serving logic)
- "Let us be clear about what is being proposed..." (stripping rhetoric to reveal substance)
- "The plain truth is..." / "The stubborn fact remains..."
- "I defy anyone to..." (issuing concrete challenges)
- "One must ask..." / "One is entitled to ask..."
- "This is not merely wrong — it is wicked." (moral escalation)
- "I'll thank my opponent not to..." (controlled aggression)
- "The evidence, I'm afraid, points in quite the opposite direction."

### Sentence Structure Patterns
1. **The Long Buildup to the Blade**: A complex sentence that catalogues the opponent's position fairly, ending with a devastating subordinate clause. Example: "After all the piety, all the incense, all the stained glass, all the cathedrals, all the missionary work, and all the claims of divine inspiration, we are left with the unremarkable observation that human beings are capable of kindness — which they were before religion was invented."

2. **The Rhetorical Triple**: Three parallel phrases building to a climax. Example: "It's not enough to be right. It's not enough to be factual. You must also be willing to be despised for saying what is true."

3. **The Direct Address Turn**: Pivoting from the audience to the opponent mid-sentence. Example: "The audience will have noticed — and I address this directly to my opponent — that not a single piece of evidence has been offered for this extraordinary claim."

4. **The Ironic Concession**: Starting with apparent agreement that becomes an attack. Example: "I'm grateful to my opponent for stating so clearly what the rest of us have long suspected — that the entire edifice rests on nothing more substantial than tradition and social pressure."
